## What changed my thinking

<br>

<table>
<tr>
<td width="50%" valign="top">

### On fairness

Reading **Arvind Narayanan's**<br>*"What If Algorithmic Fairness Is a Category Error?"* alongside a hiring-algorithm case study.

Fairness isn't a technical property waiting to be optimized — it involves competing values, historical context, and human judgment that no model resolves on its own.

</td>
<td width="50%" valign="top">

### On disagreement

Reading **Susan Benesch's** work on counterspeech via the [Dangerous Speech Project](https://www.dangerousspeech.org/).

AI should amplify human effort, not replace it. Trust, empathy, and civic participation are human qualities — and respectful disagreement is often *necessary* for progress, not an obstacle to it.

</td>
</tr>
</table>

## Three commitments going forward

<br>

**1 · Stay skeptical of simple solutions**
Technical systems improve efficiency. They cannot resolve questions that are fundamentally social, political, or moral — and pretending otherwise is its own kind of risk.

**2 · Seek perspectives beyond engineering**
Responsible technology requires policymakers, social scientists, and the communities a system actually affects — not just the people who built it.

**3 · Measure success by what technology supports, not just what it enables**
Progress should strengthen trust, dignity, and participation — not simply maximize capability.

## About

This is a personal statement of practice written on completion of **[Ethics, Technology + Public Policy for Practitioners (ETPP)](https://ethicsinsociety.stanford.edu/tech-ethics-practitioner-programs)** at Stanford University — a cohort-based program for technologists, policymakers, and civil society practitioners examining the intersection of ethics, technology, and public policy.

The essay traces a shift in how I think about technical work: from treating capability as the primary measure of progress, to treating ethical reflection as inseparable from the design process itself. It draws specifically on two ideas from the program that reshaped that thinking — **Arvind Narayanan's critique of algorithmic fairness as a category error**, and **Susan Benesch's work on counterspeech**, via the Dangerous Speech Project.

📄 **[Read the full reflection →](./personal-statement-of-practice.md)**

<br>

## Core themes

| Theme | What it means in practice |
|---|---|
| **Fairness isn't a metric** | Fairness involves competing values and historical context that no model can fully resolve — only make visible. |
| **Ethics belongs at the start** | Ethical reflection is part of the design process, not a checkpoint applied after a system is already built. |
| **Disagreement is productive** | Respectful disagreement is often necessary for good decisions, not an obstacle to them. |
| **Humans aren't substitutable** | AI can support trust, empathy, and civic participation — it can't replace the human qualities behind them. |

<br>

## Three commitments

1. **Remain skeptical of simple solutions** — technical systems can't resolve questions that are fundamentally social, political, or moral.
2. **Seek perspectives beyond engineering** — responsible technology requires policymakers, social scientists, and the communities affected by these systems.
3. **Measure success by what technology supports, not just what it enables** — progress should strengthen trust, dignity, and participation.
